Research

I am an experimental particle physicists at the Fermi National Accelerator Laboratory (Fermilab) studying neutrinos to understand the nature and origin of our universe.

Neutrino Physics

Neutrinos are abundant yet elusive fundamental particles which play a key role in the weak interactions, first described by Enrico Fermi. While we may not be directly aware of their presence, neutrinos are involved in many processes which contribute to making our universe the way it is. Beta-decay from natural radioactivity, nuclear fusion reactions needed to power the stars, violent cosmic explosions such as supernovae, and the decay of particles produced by cosmic-ray showers are all examples of how this particle manifests itself.

Detector R&D

Progress in particle physics goes hand in hand with advances in detector technology. Throughout my work I have been involved in the construction, commissioning, operation and development of a specific technology, the Liquid Argon Time Projection Chamber (LArTPC), which measures light and ionization produced by charged particles traversing large volumes of argon in order to image neutrino interactions in five dimensions (position, time, and energy). I am interested in learning how to best make use of this detector, developing novel calibration techniques, as well as working to push this technology further.
